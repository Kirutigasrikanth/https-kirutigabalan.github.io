Overview

Building microfluidic devices for controlled electrochemical experiments.

I designed and fabricated microfluidic cells to enable controlled electrochemical experiments — most notably for electro-extraction of copper from dilute electrolyte, with in-situ optical visualization of deposition patterns.

The work spanned two fabrication tracks: photolithography on glass for precision channels requiring in-situ observation, and soft lithography with PDMS cast on 3D-printed molds for rapid design iteration. Both tracks surfaced non-trivial process failures that required structured DOE-based troubleshooting to resolve — the kind of work that translates directly to wafer-scale fabrication.

Track 1 — Photolithography

Glass microfluidic cell with 10 µm channels for quasi-2D transport experiments.

The goal was a quasi-2D transport environment: a 10 µm-deep channel between copper electrodes on a glass substrate, enabling 2D planar deposition and in-situ optical visualization of dendrite growth.

Step 01

Spin Coating

  • Uniform SU-8 at 25 ± 3 µm target thickness
  • Thickness governed by t = kp² / w1/2
  • Watchouts: edge bead, striations, non-uniformity
  • Soft bake to drive off solvent
Step 02

Mask Alignment & Exposure

  • Negative photoresist (SU-8) on SUSS MA6
  • Nominal dose 240 mJ/cm², window ±10–15%
  • Soft contact mode for resolution
  • Process adapted for glass transmission differences
Step 03

Development + PEB

  • SU-8 developer → IPA rinse → N₂ dry
  • Temperature ramp and dwell time controlled
  • Failure modes: incomplete crosslinking, overbake distortion
Step 04

HF Etching

  • 6% HF at 25 °C, ~0.6 µm/min etch rate
  • 10 µm depth reached in ~17 min
  • Stopped by DI rinse; depth verified by profilometer
  • Failure modes: undercut, delamination, non-uniform depth
Technical Challenge

SU-8 delamination during HF etch

SU-8 lift-off during HF exposure was causing widened channels, poor edge definition, and loss of run-to-run repeatability. The target: eliminate delamination and achieve < 2 µm feature deviation with stable masking.

Hypotheses

  • Adhesion failure at the SU-8 / glass interface
  • Incomplete crosslinking of the resist
  • Limited mask durability under prolonged HF exposure

DOE — Factors Screened

FactorLowHigh
Dehydration bake10 min20 min
Exposure dose220 mJ/cm²260 mJ/cm²
Hard bake temp120 °C150 °C

Responses: delamination (pass/fail), channel depth (Dektak 6M profilometer), feature integrity (Zeiss Axioscope 2 MAT optical microscope).

Findings

  • Adhesion had the strongest correlation with mask survival
  • Increased dehydration bake time reduced delamination
  • Higher-than-nominal exposure energy improved crosslinking

Track 2 — Soft Lithography

PDMS cast on 3D-printed molds for rapid prototyping and flow studies.

For rapid prototyping, I developed a PDMS-based process using 3D-printed molds to study the effect of flow parameters on extraction efficiency. Channel depth of 10 µm was maintained using SLA 3D printing with a resin and printer rated to 1 µm resolution.

Step 01

Design & Print Mold

  • Geometry designed in CAD
  • SLA printed at 1 µm resolution
  • Includes slots for channel and electrodes
Step 02

Cast & Cure PDMS

  • PDMS poured onto mold, degassed under vacuum
  • Controlled curing to maintain dimensional fidelity
Step 03

Plasma Bonding to Glass

  • March RIE plasma etcher
  • RF 105 W, 30% O₂, tuned pressure and time
  • Surface activation via Si–OH generation
Step 04

Leak Testing

  • Flow validation of bonded device
  • Go / no-go gate before electrochemical testing
Technical Challenge

PDMS–glass bonding failure at 60 s plasma exposure

Devices were failing leak tests — weak adhesion and visible leakage after 60 s of O₂ plasma. The working hypothesis: prolonged exposure was over-oxidizing the surface and collapsing the effective density of Si–OH bonding sites.

Approach

  • Systematically reduced plasma exposure time from 60 s → 20 s
  • Evaluated bond quality across the sweep
  • Isolated a stable window at 22 ± 2 s

Outcome

22 ± 2 s Optimized plasma exposure
0 leaks After optimization
SOP Standardized process created

Translating to Wafer Fabrication

How these methods scale naturally to high-volume semiconductor processing.

Process Optimization

Apply DOE to define clean process windows for lithography and etch steps, bounding each factor to a stable region.

Yield Improvement

Use SPC (X-MR, I-MR charts) to monitor process stability, detect drift early, and reduce wafer-to-wafer and run-to-run variability.

Troubleshooting

Structured root-cause analysis, distinguishing tool-induced from process-induced failures so fixes target the right system.
