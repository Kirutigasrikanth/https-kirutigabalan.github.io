CFD Modeling of Microvascular Flow Phantom

Problem: Studying microcirculation and blood perfusion requires a controlled platform that can mimic biological microvessels and generate reliable flow conditions for validation. The challenge was to design a compact phantom-based setup and verify whether the modeled flow stayed within the laminar regime relevant to microvasculature.

Method: Designed the syringe-pump-driven experimental setup and flow phantom architecture in CAD, then imported the geometry into ANSYS Workbench and FLUENT for simulation. Used adaptive coarse meshing with tetrahedral elements to balance accuracy and computational speed, and solved the pressure-driven flow using the Navier–Stokes equations with second-order accuracy and the SIMPLE algorithm.

  • Developed CAD models for the syringe pump and full experimental setup
  • Built and meshed a microchannel flow phantom for CFD analysis
  • Evaluated velocity contours, cell Reynolds number, and wall shear stress
  • Connected simulation outputs to physiologically relevant flow diagnostics

Results: The simulated flow remained in the laminar regime, with Reynolds number below 2000 throughout the phantom. Post-processing also produced velocity and wall shear stress distributions that can be used to study flow behavior and distinguish healthy versus diseased microvascular conditions.

Method: Implemented a Single Particle Model with coupled stress analysis using numerical solvers for DAEs. Applied Crank–Nicholson methods for stability and accuracy.
